# § Erratum and Reformulation — *U-Mentalism Patent* (IJWesT 12.1, 2021)

*Filed under Tabula 02 — Computer Architecture & Photonics.*

This note records (a) an arithmetic erratum affecting Section 1.2 of *U-Mentalism Patent: The Beginning of Cinematic Supercomputation* (IJWesT, Vol. 12, No. 1, January 2021, DOI 10.5121/ijwest.2021.12101), and (b) a reformulation of the quantitative comparison with the TOP500 supercomputer benchmark presented in Section 1.4. The architectural and ontological propositions of the paper (Sections 1.1–1.3) are unaffected by what follows; the comparative claim is here restated on a parameterised footing that, in the present author's view, is in fact stronger than the original.

## (i) Arithmetic erratum: bytes-to-Terabytes conversion in §1.2

The data volume of one hour of an 8K (7680 × 4320) film at 60 FPS and 24 bits per pixel is correctly derived in the paper up to:

$$60 \times 60 \times 60 \times 99{,}532{,}800 \text{ bytes} \;=\; 21{,}499{,}084{,}800{,}000 \text{ bytes} \;\approx\; 2.15 \times 10^{13} \text{ bytes/hour.}$$

The paper then states this value as

> "2.1499085e+25 Terabytes, or 21.499085 × 10¹⁵ Zettabytes."

The correct conversion is

$$2.15 \times 10^{13} \text{ bytes} \;\div\; 10^{12} \;=\; 21.5 \text{ TB,}$$

equivalently $\approx 2.15 \times 10^{-8}$ ZB. The discrepancy originated in the conversion from bytes to Terabytes, where the byte count was *multiplied* by $10^{12}$ instead of *divided* by it. The subsequent TB → ZB step was performed correctly, which gave the chain a misleading appearance of internal consistency. The accumulated factor of error is $10^{24}$. Henceforth, the value to be retained as the data volume of one hour of one 8K / 60 FPS / 24-bit film is **≈ 21.5 TB**.

## (ii) Figures whose reading is here corrected

The following figures, which derive from the value addressed in (i), are here corrected:

The figure reported as "1.2285191e+14 times more than the expected global data for 2025" is to be read as **≈ 1.23 × 10⁻¹⁰** of the projected 2025 datasphere; equivalently, the 2025 datasphere of 175 ZB (IDC/Seagate) is **≈ 8.14 × 10⁹ times** the data volume of one hour of one film.

The estimates of "120 years" and "17,500 years" of accumulated global data needed to match one hour of U-Mentalism reverse direction once the corrected figure is substituted: a single node operating at the stated parameters would require approximately $8.1 \times 10^9$ hours — about 930,000 years — to generate a data volume equal to the projected 2025 datasphere. The present reflection records this inversion, which the conservative parametrization in (iii) below addresses by way of network aggregation rather than by per-node throughput.

The internal arithmetic of Section 1.4 reading "21.499085 × 10¹⁵ Zettabytes ÷ 8,266,752 = 2.6006689e+15 Zettabyes, which converted is 2.6006689e+15 Petabytes" additionally exhibits a division off by a factor of $10^6$ and a unit ambiguity (the same numerical value written as both ZB and PB). The reformulation in (iii) below provides the basis on which per-node and aggregate throughput are to be computed instead.

## (iii) Reformulated comparison with TOP500 systems

The original Section 1.4 implicitly equated bytes of (typical) digital image data with floating-point operations. The architectural justification for this equivalence — the data/processing entanglement intrinsic to U-Mentalism, in which each pixel is simultaneously storage cell and processing cell, dissolving the von Neumann bottleneck — is here made explicit, by introducing the local processor clock and the per-frame parallelism as distinct multiplicative factors.

Let:

- $p$ = number of pixel-processors per frame; for the 8K reference, $p = 7680 \times 4320 = 3.32 \times 10^7$,
- $f$ = local clock rate per pixel-processor (Hz),
- $k$ = symbolic operations per cycle per pixel-processor (canonical: $k = 1$),
- $N$ = number of cooperating UTM-nodes on the network.

The aggregate symbolic throughput of the system is then

$$\boxed{\;\text{Ops/s} \;=\; N \cdot p \cdot f \cdot k\;}$$

The four factors are independently grounded: $p$ by the frame resolution; $f$ by the local clock of the UTM (the byte being a fixed unit, the temporal dimension is contributed by $f$); $k$ by the symbolic atom adopted (one cycle per pixel-state transition, in the canonical case); $N$ by the cardinality of the cooperating network on the Internet.

### Conservative parametrization (principal claim)

In the **conservative parametrization** — taking the local clock equal to the frame rate, $f = 60$ Hz, with $k = 1$ and $N = 5 \times 10^{10}$ (current order of magnitude of Internet-connected nodes) — each node contributes

$$p \cdot f \cdot k \;\approx\; 3.32 \times 10^7 \times 60 \;\approx\; 2 \times 10^9 \text{ ops/s} \;\approx\; 2 \text{ GFLOPS-equivalent,}$$

and the aggregate is

$$N \cdot p \cdot f \cdot k \;\approx\; 10^{20} \text{ ops/s} \;\approx\; 100 \text{ ExaFLOPS-equivalent.}$$

This conservative figure assumes **no local clock above the frame rate** and so derives entirely from the spatial parallelism of the frame. It already stands at approximately **57 × the current TOP500 No. 1 system** — El Capitan at Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory, measured at 1.742 ExaFLOPS in the 66th edition of the TOP500 (November 2025) — and approximately **241 × the Fugaku** system as cited in the 2021 paper (415 PFLOPS in 2020; the system, now upgraded to 442 PFLOPS, holds the No. 4 position in the 66th TOP500). The conservative case is offered as the principal lower bound for the architectural claim.

### Illustrative scaling with local clock

For any realistic silicon implementation, the local pixel-processor will admit a clock above the frame rate, and the aggregate scales linearly in $f$. As an illustration only:

| $f$ | per node | aggregate ($N = 5 \times 10^{10}$) | factor × El Capitan |
|---|---|---|---|
| 60 Hz | 2 GFLOPS-eq. | 100 EFLOPS-eq. | ≈ 57 × |
| 1 MHz | 33 TFLOPS-eq. | $1.67 \times 10^{9}$ PFLOPS-eq. | ≈ $10^6$ × |
| 1 GHz | 33 PFLOPS-eq. | $1.67 \times 10^{12}$ PFLOPS-eq. | ≈ $10^9$ × |

### Note on the restatement

The qualitative claim of "computation at or near the speed of light" is preserved and is here expressed as a scaling law over four declared parameters $(p, f, k, N)$ rather than as an undeclared identification of bytes with floating-point operations. Of these, $p$ and $N$ are matters of physical fact about the implementation and the network; $f$ and $k$ are design choices to be specified per realisation. The present formulation makes the source of the computational surplus visible (parallelism × clock × cooperation), distinguishes it from von Neumann supercomputing on a principled basis, and allows the comparison with TOP500 systems to be parameterised and reproduced.
